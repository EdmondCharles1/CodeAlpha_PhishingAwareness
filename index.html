What is Phishing? 🎣

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where attackers disguise themselves as trustworthy entities to trick you into revealing sensitive information — passwords, credit card numbers, personal data, or login credentials.

Types of Phishing

📧 Email Phishing

Mass emails pretending to be from banks, companies, or services you use. The most common type.

🎯 Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks aimed at a specific person or organization, using personal details to seem legitimate.

🐋 Whaling

Spear phishing targeting high-level executives (CEOs, CFOs). The "big fish" of phishing.

💬 Smishing

Phishing via SMS text messages. "Your package is delayed, click here to track it."

📞 Vishing

Voice phishing through phone calls. Attackers pretend to be tech support, banks, or government.

🌐 Clone Phishing

A legitimate email is cloned and resent with malicious links replacing the original ones.

How to Spot a Phishing Email 📧

Phishing emails are designed to look legitimate. Here are the red flags to watch for before clicking anything.

Anatomy of a Phishing Email

From: support@paypa1-secure.com ⚠️ Look closely: "paypa1" not "paypal" (1 instead of l)
To: you@email.com
Subject: ⚠️ URGENT: Your account will be suspended in 24 hours! ⚠️ Creates panic and urgency

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your account. Your account will be permanently suspended unless you verify your identity within 24 hours.

⚠️ Generic greeting, not your name

Click the link below to verify your account:

https://paypa1-secure.com/verify-account ⚠️ Fake URL — not the real PayPal domain

If you do not act now, your funds will be frozen and your account deleted.

⚠️ Threatening language to force quick action

Best regards,
PayPal Security Team

The 7 Red Flags

1

Suspicious sender address

Check the actual email domain. "support@paypa1.com" is not "support@paypal.com".

2

Urgency and threats

"Act now!", "Your account will be deleted!", "You have 24 hours!" — legitimate companies don't threaten you.

3

Generic greetings

"Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name. Real companies know your name.

4

Suspicious links

Hover over links before clicking. The URL should match the company's real domain.

5

Spelling and grammar errors

Professional companies proofread their emails. Multiple errors are a big red flag.

6

Requests for personal information

No legitimate company will ask for your password, SSN, or credit card via email.

7

Unexpected attachments

Don't open attachments you weren't expecting, especially .exe, .zip, or .doc files with macros.

How to Recognize Fake Websites 🌐

Phishing emails often lead to fake websites that look identical to real ones. Here's how to tell the difference before entering your information.

Spot the Fake URL

✅ Legitimate
🔒 https://www.paypal.com/login
  • ✅ Correct domain name
  • ✅ HTTPS with padlock
  • ✅ Simple, clean URL
❌ Phishing
⚠️ http://paypal-secure-login.sketchy-site.com/verify
  • ❌ "paypal" is a subdomain, not the real domain
  • ❌ No HTTPS (http only)
  • ❌ Suspicious extra words in URL

Common Tricks Attackers Use

🔤 Lookalike Domains

go0gle.com (zero instead of "o")
arnazon.com ("rn" looks like "m")
paypa1.com ("1" instead of "l")

Attackers register domains that look almost identical to real ones.

📎 Subdomain Tricks

paypal.com.evil-site.com
login.amazon.fake-domain.net

The real domain is the last part before the slash. Everything before it is a subdomain that anyone can create.

🔒 Fake HTTPS

HTTPS ≠ Safe
Attackers can get free SSL certificates

HTTPS means the connection is encrypted, not that the website is legitimate. Always check the domain name too.

⏰ Urgency Popups

"Your session expires in 2:00!"
"Account locked! Verify now!"

Fake countdown timers and alert popups pressure you into acting without thinking.

How to Verify a Website

1

Check the URL carefully

Read the domain from right to left. The real domain is right before the first single slash "/".

2

Don't click links from emails

Type the website address manually in your browser instead of clicking email links.

3

Look for poor design quality

Blurry logos, broken layouts, missing pages, or strange fonts are signs of a fake site.

4

Use a search engine

Search for the company name and use the official link from search results, not from the email.

Social Engineering Tactics 🎭

Social engineering is the art of manipulating people into giving up confidential information. Phishing is just one form of it. Understanding the psychology behind these attacks is your best defense.

The 6 Principles of Manipulation

Attackers exploit these basic human instincts to trick their victims:

⏰

Urgency

"Your account will be deleted in 1 hour!"

Why it works: Panic stops you from thinking clearly. You act before you analyze.
👔

Authority

"This is the IT department. We need your password for a security update."

Why it works: People tend to obey figures of authority without questioning.
😨

Fear

"We detected a virus on your computer. Call this number immediately."

Why it works: Fear overrides logic. Scared people make poor decisions.
🎁

Greed

"Congratulations! You've won a $1000 gift card. Claim it now!"

Why it works: The promise of free rewards makes people ignore warning signs.
🤝

Trust

"Hey, it's John from accounting. Can you send me the client list?"

Why it works: We naturally trust people who seem familiar or friendly.
❤️

Helpfulness

"I'm locked out of my account, can you let me in? I have a meeting in 5 minutes."

Why it works: Most people want to help others, especially in stressful situations.

Real-World Attack Scenarios

CEO Fraud High Risk

An employee receives an email from the "CEO" asking for an urgent wire transfer to a new vendor. The email address looks real but is slightly different: ceo@company-inc.com instead of ceo@company.com.

Result: $47 million lost — this happened to Ubiquiti Networks in 2015.
Tech Support Scam Medium Risk

A popup appears on your screen: "VIRUS DETECTED! Call Microsoft Support at 1-800-XXX". You call, and they ask you to install remote access software so they can "fix" your computer.

Result: The attacker installs malware and steals personal files, passwords, and banking info.
USB Drop Attack High Risk

An attacker drops a USB drive labeled "Salary Report 2024" in your office parking lot. A curious employee plugs it in — the USB installs a backdoor on the company network.

Result: In a University of Illinois study, 48% of people plugged in unknown USB drives they found.

How to Protect Yourself

🛑

Stop and Think

If something feels urgent, slow down. That urgency is exactly what the attacker wants.

✅

Verify Independently

Call the person or company directly using a known number, not the one in the email.

🔐

Never Share Credentials

No legitimate organization will ever ask for your password via email, phone, or chat.

📢

Report Suspicious Activity

Tell your IT team immediately. One report can prevent an entire company from being compromised.
