What is Phishing? 🎣

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where attackers disguise themselves as trustworthy entities to trick you into revealing sensitive information — passwords, credit card numbers, personal data, or login credentials.

Types of Phishing

📧 Email Phishing

Mass emails pretending to be from banks, companies, or services you use. The most common type.

🎯 Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks aimed at a specific person or organization, using personal details to seem legitimate.

🐋 Whaling

Spear phishing targeting high-level executives (CEOs, CFOs). The "big fish" of phishing.

💬 Smishing

Phishing via SMS text messages. "Your package is delayed, click here to track it."

📞 Vishing

Voice phishing through phone calls. Attackers pretend to be tech support, banks, or government.

🌐 Clone Phishing

A legitimate email is cloned and resent with malicious links replacing the original ones.

How to Spot a Phishing Email 📧

Phishing emails are designed to look legitimate. Here are the red flags to watch for before clicking anything.

Anatomy of a Phishing Email

From: support@paypa1-secure.com ⚠️ Look closely: "paypa1" not "paypal" (1 instead of l)
To: you@email.com
Subject: ⚠️ URGENT: Your account will be suspended in 24 hours! ⚠️ Creates panic and urgency

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your account. Your account will be permanently suspended unless you verify your identity within 24 hours.

⚠️ Generic greeting, not your name

Click the link below to verify your account:

https://paypa1-secure.com/verify-account ⚠️ Fake URL — not the real PayPal domain

If you do not act now, your funds will be frozen and your account deleted.

⚠️ Threatening language to force quick action

Best regards,
PayPal Security Team

The 7 Red Flags

1

Suspicious sender address

Check the actual email domain. "support@paypa1.com" is not "support@paypal.com".

2

Urgency and threats

"Act now!", "Your account will be deleted!", "You have 24 hours!" — legitimate companies don't threaten you.

3

Generic greetings

"Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name. Real companies know your name.

4

Suspicious links

Hover over links before clicking. The URL should match the company's real domain.

5

Spelling and grammar errors

Professional companies proofread their emails. Multiple errors are a big red flag.

6

Requests for personal information

No legitimate company will ask for your password, SSN, or credit card via email.

7

Unexpected attachments

Don't open attachments you weren't expecting, especially .exe, .zip, or .doc files with macros.
